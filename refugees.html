The number of globally displaced people is rising to levels not seen since World War II.

Data show the displaced population has more than tripled in the last decade:

Total Displaced Peoples, 2000 - 2015

Who are these people?

The above chart shows the growth of four different types of displaced people.

Refugees, as defined by the UN High Comission on Refugees, is any person "who has been forced to flee his or her country because of persecution, war, or violence [and] has a well-founded fear of persecution for reasons of race, religion, nationality, political opinion or membership in a particular social group".

Internally displaced people (IDP) have also been displaced from their home like a refugee, but still reside in their home country. Unlike refugees, IDPs are not protected by international law or eligible to receive many types of aid.

Stateless people are those who for one reason or another are denied citizenship from any nation. This limits them from accessing basic rights like education and healthcare.

Others of concern are people who "do not necessarily fall directly into any of the groups above but to whom UNHCR has extended its protection and/or assistance services, based on humanitarian or other special grounds".

This data comes from a collection by the Humanitarian Data Exchange and the UNHCR's most recent Global Trends report.

Who is taking in these refugees?

A select few countries are absorbing recent increases

Total Refugees by Residence, 2000 - 2015

In the last five years, significant growths in refugee populations have been created by the Syrian civil war, adding to the existing conflicts in the region. A small number of countries in the region have played a large role in taking these refugees, such as Turkey, Jordan and Lebanon.

So far, Western countries have played a fairly small role in taking in these recent increases. The United States in particular has not significantly increased its refugee population in the last 10 years.

For a more complete view of inflows and outflows, as well as news explaining individual refugee crises, you should visit the excellent Refugee Project.

How can distribution of refugees be more equitable?

Countries near conflicts are clearly the most convenient hosts for refugees, but they are often ill-equipped to do so.

Richer countries that are farther away often simply do not want to host refugees in any significant number. Vetting processes are long and arduous, and the total number accepted is often quite small.

The main explainer of number of refugees is in geography - those closest to conflicts will take in the most refugees. There are eight countries that host more than 400,000 refugees:

  • Turkey, Jordan, and Lebanon neighbor Syria.
  • Pakistan and Iran neighbor Afghanistan.
  • Ethiopia, Kenya, and Uganda host refugees from conflicts in nearby Sudan, Somalia and Democratic Republic of the Congo.

Unfortunately, most of these countries rank fairly low in economic metrics.

Many richer countries take in a fair number of refugees, but do not generally have policies in place that would significantly alleviate the poorer host countries. There are also a few richer countries who do a particularly bad job:

  • There is disagreement about whether or not rich Gulf states are accepting refugees. Countries like Saudi Arabia and UAE are not signatories of the UNHCR convention, and therefore do not provide data. However, they have contended that they are aiding Syrians in other ways.
  • In 2015, Japan rejected 99% of refugees and has only accepted six Syrian refugees.
  • Brazil's political scandals have halted its plans to accept 100,000 Syrian refugees over five years.
  • Australia has only managed to settle one sixth of its modest settlement goal.
